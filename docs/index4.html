In the Wake of COVID-19, Community Gardens Transform Neighborhoods

by Imani Sebri

"IMAGINING THE COLLECTIVE," A community garden bringing people together / Danie Drankwalter.

Community gardens are a beautiful addition to any neighborhood. But how can comunity gardens transform the landscape of marginalized communities?

October 3, 2021 11:59pm

It goes without saying that the pandemic has disrupted our lives. It has restructured how we work, socialize, and cultivate community. It has also exacerbated long-standing prevalent issues such as food insecurity, or the lack of access to fresh, nutritional foods due to location, socioeconomic status, among other reasons.

Here in New York City, the Bronx has the highest rate of food insecurity in the boroughs, with nearly 230,000 residents facing some form of it. This not just an urban issue, either. In more rural areas like Mississippi, food insecurity still runs rampant, effecting over 700,000 residents.

And as you can imagine, a global pandemic hasn‚Äôt helped much. Feeding America, an organization dedicated to addressing food insecurity in the United States, projected that more than 42 million people, 13 million of which are children, experienced food insecurity in 2020 due to COVID-19. Additionally, by May 2020 nearly 20.5 million people filed for unemployment, and when unemployment rises so do people's ability to afford food.

Perhaps lesser known consquences of the pandemic include disrupted food production and food shortages, compounding how communities experience food insecurity. With all fo this in mind, So what can be done?

In recent years, community gardens have gained traction in cities across the United States. They serve as green spaces for communities to grow their own fresh and healthy food, learn about urban agriculture, and organize and advocate for themselves as a community. Gil Lopez, an urban farmer and guerilla gardener from Western Queens, spoke at length about how community gardens function as a democratic and collaborative space.
